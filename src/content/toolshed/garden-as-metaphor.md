The word "garden" is doing real work here. It's not decoration — it's the operating philosophy behind how content is created, published, and maintained.

## Stream vs garden

The web's default mode is the stream: a reverse-chronological feed where new content pushes old content out of sight. Streams reward frequency and recency. A post from three years ago is effectively invisible.

A garden is different. It's topological, not chronological — ideas connect to other ideas based on what they're about, not when they were planted. A post from two years ago and one from last week can sit side by side if they're about the same thing.

## Ideas as living things

The core claim: ideas aren't finished or unfinished. They're at different stages of growth. A 🌱 seedling is real — it exists, it might matter — it's just not a tree yet. Publishing a seedling isn't failure to finish; it's an invitation to watch something grow.

This reframes the publishing question. Instead of "is this ready?" the question becomes "where is this in its growth?" Every post answers that question visibly with the maturity track.

## What tending means

A gardener doesn't plant once and leave. They prune, water, revisit. The garden has an `updated` field precisely because posts can and should be revised. The "Tended [date]" marker on cards and post headers signals that this is living writing, not a historical record.

The `pruning` field on articles is even more literal — notes on what to cut when the time comes.

## Thinking in public

Gardens give permission to publish unfinished things. The maturity track makes the unfinishedness explicit and legible, so a 🌱 post isn't read as sloppy — it's read as early. This is different from a blog, where every post implies the same level of completion.

Publishing incomplete thoughts invites collaboration, surfaces blind spots, and creates accountability to develop them further. The garden is a thinking space, not a broadcast channel.

## Why "tended" not "updated"

Language matters. "Updated" is mechanical — a timestamp. "Tended" implies care. It's the same reason posts are "planted" not "published," collections are "seeds" not "drafts," and the maturity stages are plant emojis not version numbers. The metaphor is consistent all the way down.

## The weeds question

The garden metaphor also asks hard questions. A real garden has weeds — content that's no longer accurate, no longer you, or superseded by newer thinking. The `pruning` field is the beginning of an answer. A fuller lifecycle — greenhouse for incoming content, compost for what decays, soil for what breaks down into raw material for new seeds — sketches the rest. That's still being worked out.
