## The seed

It started with a blank Astro project and a question: what if my writing didn't have to be a blog?

Blogs are chronological. They push old posts down and reward recency over relevance. I wanted something that worked more like my brain: connected, evolving, comfortable with incompleteness. A [[digital-gardens-vs-blogs|digital garden]].

## Day one: the foundation

The initial commit set up the core architecture. Astro 5 with static output, deployed to GitHub Pages. Seven content collections covering different types of thinking:

- **Articles**: long-form pieces, many migrated from my Substack
- **Field Notes**: observations and reflections (like this one)
- **Sparks**: seeds of ideas, not yet grown into full pieces
- **Weblinks**: annotated bookmarks to things I've found valuable
- **Videos**: talks and recordings worth revisiting
- **Library**: books I'm reading or have read
- **Principles**: beliefs I try to work by

Each collection has its own personality, but they all share common DNA: a title, a date, tags, a maturity stage, and a description.

## The maturity system

One of the first design decisions: every piece of content has a growth stage. Not "published" or "draft", but a spectrum:

- 🌱 **Seedling**: just planted, barely more than an idea
- 🌿 **Sprout**: starting to take shape
- 🪴 **Plant**: solid, mostly formed
- 🌳 **Tree**: mature, complete (for now)

This matters because it signals to readers that imperfection is intentional. A seedling isn't broken. It's growing.

## Content migration

Importing years of Substack articles happened in batches. Over 40 articles spanning 2020 to 2025, each needing frontmatter, image paths, and sometimes a "pruning" reflection: a note from present-me looking back at what past-me wrote. What aged well? What didn't?

## Publishing from anywhere

A CMS layer (Sveltia CMS) and a GitHub Actions workflow made it possible to add content from a phone. See an interesting link? Share it to the garden. The workflow auto-fetches the page title and creates a weblink entry. A `publish.bat` script handles the local git dance.

## The design system

The visual identity came together around a few principles:

- **Hot pink (#D6006C)** as the accent color. My company color. Non-negotiable.
- **Lora** for headings (warm, serif) and **Roboto** for body text (clean, readable)
- Earthy pastel tones for collection colors: warm browns, muted greens, soft oranges
- A hand-drawn aesthetic using SVG `feTurbulence` filters that make icons look slightly wobbly, like they were sketched

The icons were a particular joy. Each collection has its own hand-drawn SVG: a beaker for field notes, a lightbulb for sparks, a pen nib for articles. They use thin strokes with rounded caps and the same wobble filter, so everything feels organic and personal.

## Wiki links and connections

The garden is a network, not a list. Wiki-style `[[double bracket]]` links connect ideas across collections. Hover over a link and you see a preview card with the title and description. Every page also tracks its backlinks: which other pages link to it.

This creates a web of connections that grows richer as more content is added. A field note might link to an article, which links to a principle, which connects to a spark. You can follow your curiosity.

## AI transparency

If an AI helped write something, the reader should know. Every piece of content can declare its AI involvement level:

- **No AI**: fully written by me
- **AI-assisted**: my ideas and structure, AI helped refine
- **Co-created with AI**: written by AI based on my ideas and direction
- **AI-generated**: fully generated by AI, reviewed by me

This shows up as a small labeled box at the top of the post. Transparency over pretending.

## The connection map

The latest addition: a visual sidebar showing how each post connects to the rest of the garden. It renders as a small SVG with the current post as a center node and lines radiating outward to related and linked posts.

Each node is color-coded by collection. The lines have a slow flowing animation. The center gently pulses. The nodes drift slightly, like leaves in a breeze. It's not a full knowledge graph, just a small, warm reminder that no idea exists in isolation.

The sidebar also shows tags, backlinks, and related content, making it easy to keep exploring.

## What I learned

Building in public with an AI assistant is a conversation. Every feature started as a vague intention and became concrete through iteration. Some things I thought I wanted turned out wrong (aggressive em-dash replacement, overly polished icons). Some things I didn't know I wanted became essential (the maturity system, the connection map).

The best part: the garden isn't finished. It's designed not to be. Every visit is a chance to plant something new, water something old, or pull out something that no longer belongs.
